[UNIT HIERARCHY 10]
[VAR55]:\nYou are in command of a single unit, that is attached to a higher HQ. Your unit will move as part of it on the campaign map. Your management options are limited to this one unit. You will recognize your unit from a brighter unit (NATO-) symbol in the Management -panel. Find your unit on the map by clicking the arrow button in the career panel in top left of the screen.\nYou will lead the unit in battles, receiving orders from your higher-ups. Carrying out these orders and inflicting casualties will increase your prestige.\nYou may advice units on the campaign map to move using your prestige. You may also try to influence Policy and Project choices, but due to your low position, it's expensive.

[UNIT HIERARCHY 14]
[VAR55]:\nNow you're in command of a HQ, which can have multiple infantry, cavalry or artillery units attached to it. You can manage these units, including recruitment of new ones.\nYour HQ is attached to a higher HQ, and operates as a part of it on the campaign map. You may influence decisions of your commanding officer and others using prestige.\nDuring battles, you will command your unit and will receive orders from your commanding officer. You will gain prestige, fame and experience from actions of all units under your command.\nYou may also try to influence Policy and Project choices, but due to your relatively low position, it's still expensive.
[UNIT HIERARCHY 15]
[VAR55]:\nYour unit is now present on the campaign map, and you can move it freely. You can also manage all units attached to it, including creation of new HQs and recruitment of units. You will personally command the unit in battles.\nLike earlier, you may use prestige to influence commanders of other units to follow your advice. 
[UNIT HIERARCHY 16]
[VAR55]:\nIn addition to moving your HQ on the campaign map, you can have multiple independently operating sub-unit HQs directly under your command. You can manage and move them all freely on the campaign map.\nIf any of your sub-units engage in battle, you can take command of them in the field. Only if you are personally present in the battle, you will receive full prestige from combat actions.\nInfluencing commanders not under your command is still possible, as is influencing Projects and Policies. These actions cost less prestige due to your high position. 
[UNIT HIERARCHY 18]
[VAR55]:\nNow you're in such high position, that on the campaign map you can have multiple HQs with their subordinate HQs directly and freely movable under your command for improved coordination. You can manage all the subordinate units, including all reorganizing as you see fit. Your [VAR55] HQ is no longer directly movable on the campaign map, but it can be selected for further information about the command bonus it will provide to all attached units.\nWhen any of your subordinate units engage the enemy, you can take command of the battle. Due to your high position, you are no longer represented as a unit in the battle field, but rather coordinate the operations through your subordinates.\nYour attributes directly affect all your subordinate commanders' performance through your command style and leadership. Prestige, experience and fame are generated from actions of any of your units.
[UNIT HIERARCHY - Commanding General]
CONGRATULATIONS, SIR!\nYou are now the highest military commander of your nation, appointed by the President himself. This is the highest honor an officer can receive. You should live up to the expectations - if removed from command, it could ruin your career.\n\nAs the Commanding General (Union) or General in Chief (CSA) you have full control over all the armies and fleets of your nation. This means you are required to give the orders on the campaign map, and to manage the armies/fleets, including recruitment. You will still retain a personal command over the unit of your choosing.\nYou are in charge of weapon procurement for your armies, and can place orders in the Weapons -panel under Management.\nInfluencing Policies and Projects is now cheaper due to your high position.\nNational morale effects from battles you are personally involved in are higher than from other battles.
